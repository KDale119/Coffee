Coffee

Coffee grown worldwide can trace its heritage back centuries to the ancient coffee forests on the Ethiopian plateau. There, legend says the goat herder Kaldi first discovered the potential of these beloved beans.

The story goes that that Kaldi discovered coffee after he noticed that after eating the berries from a certain tree, his goats became so energetic that they did not want to sleep at night.

Kaldi reported his findings to the abbot of the local monastery, who made a drink with the berries and found that it kept him alert through the long hours of evening prayer. The abbot shared his discovery with the other monks at the monastery, and knowledge of the energizing berries began to spread.

As word moved east and coffee reached the Arabian Peninsula, it began a journey which would bring these beans across the globe.

Coffea arabica is today grown in dozens of countries between the Tropic of Capricorn and the Tropic of Cancer. Wild plants grow between 30 and 39 ft tall and have an open branching system. The leaves are opposite, simple elliptic-ovate to oblong 2.5–4.5 in long and 1.5–3 in a dark glossy green. The flowers are white, 10–15 mm in diameter and grow in clusters.

Three of Colombia's most distinguished coffees—Medellin, Armenia, and Manizales are named after the region in which they were grown and then often marketed together to simplify the transfers of large coffee contracts. These coffees are known by the acronym MAM.

Robusta coffee refers to coffee that comes from the plant species Coffea canephora. Coffea canephora is one of the two primary species of coffee plants cultivated around our world, the other being Coffea arabica, or “Arabica” coffee. Though Arabica used to dominate global coffee production over Robusta by a wide margin—around 80/20—these days, Robusta now makes up a little more than 40% of coffee grown worldwide, according to World Coffee Research.

One of the oldest, simplest, fastest and cheapest ways to brew coffee is the drip method using a coffee cone and paper filter. Hot water is poured evenly over coffee grounds in a paper filter. With gravity, the brewed coffee drips slowly and directly into a cup or pot.

The French Press method, invented in 1929, extracts arguably more superior flavors than any other method. In a press pot, ground coffee is soaked, steeped and strained in hot water; therefore, coffee’s flavorful essential oils, caffeine and antioxidants are better diffused and preserved leaving the purest flavors of the coffee.

Espresso is not a type of coffee bean or a roasting style – a common misconception. It's a process of brewing coffee and is instead made by forcing high-pressured hot water through very finely ground coffee beans. This is then topped with a crema, a brown foam, that adds the rich, full-flavored aftertaste.
